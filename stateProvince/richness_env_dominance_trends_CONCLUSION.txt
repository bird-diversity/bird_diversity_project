What this notebook does (in plain English)
------------------------------------------
• Bird records were cleaned and linked to environmental data (vegetation, land cover, light, climate, 
elevation, aerosols, etc.).

• To avoid counting the same place many times, the data were “thinned”: within each state and each 5 km 
grid cell, we kept one record per species. That gives a fairer picture of how many species appear in each 
area without duplicate visits dominating the numbers.

• For each grid cell we calculated species richness (how many bird species) and average environmental values. 
Land cover was summarized as the most common land cover class in that cell. NDVI (greenness) was always 
considered together with land cover, because the same NDVI number can mean different things in forest, crop, 
or built-up areas.

• We compared richness across states, looked at how richness relates to the environment within each state and 
overall, fitted a statistical model (Poisson regression) for richness, measured how “uneven” bird communities 
are in each state (dominance), and looked at simple long-term trends in yearly counts for common species.


Main results from the overall richness model (Poisson GLM)
----------------------------------------------------------
These results are from the model that predicts species richness per grid cell using NDVI, land cover type, night 
light (avg_rad), elevation, temperature, rainfall, and aerosol extinction — on about 1,700 grid cells after 
dropping rare land cover classes.

• NDVI: After accounting for land cover and the other variables, higher NDVI is associated with slightly lower 
expected richness in this model (negative coefficient; statistically significant at the usual 5% level in the 
saved run). This should be read carefully: NDVI is not “bad for birds” by itself; it interacts with habitat type, 
and the model is associational, not proof of cause.

• Night-time radiance (avg_rad): Higher values are associated with higher richness in this model (positive 
coefficient; statistically significant). This often tracks human activity and sampling — more people reporting 
birds in brighter areas — so it is partly an effort effect, not only ecology.

• Temperature (temp_mean): Higher temperature is associated with lower expected richness in this model (negative 
coefficient; statistically significant).

• Elevation, rainfall, and total aerosol extinction: In this fitted model they were not clearly different from 
zero (not statistically significant in the saved output). That does not prove they “don’t matter”; they may be 
correlated with other variables or vary too little in the sample.

• Land cover: Several land cover classes differ from the reference class in the model (some negative associations 
were significant in the saved run). The numeric codes should be mapped back to your land cover legend when you 
write them up.

• The model fit is reasonably strong for a descriptive Poisson model (high pseudo R-squared in the saved output), 
but it describes patterns in the data — it does not prove that changing one factor would change bird richness.


Other parts of the notebook (short summary)
-------------------------------------------
• Richness by state: States differ in both average richness per cell and total number of species recorded. 
Maps and bars show where diversity is higher or lower under the thinning rules used here.

• Within-state environment: Correlations between richness and environment can differ from one state to another. 
That means a relationship seen for the whole country is not always the same in every region.

• Dominance: Some states have bird communities dominated by a few very common species; others are more even. 
Berger–Parker, Simpson, Shannon, and Pielou metrics summarize that.

• Trends in counts over years: For species with enough years of data, we fitted simple trends in log(total 
count per year). These trends mix real change with more reporting and more eBird (or similar) use over time. 
Treat them as exploratory, not as firm population estimates.


Important limitations (keep these in any paper or report)
---------------------------------------------------------
• Counts and lists reflect where and when people looked for birds, not a complete census.

• “Correlation” and regression here show association, not causation.

• Yearly trends are confounded with survey effort and technology.

• NDVI must be interpreted with land cover; the notebook’s figures and model are built that way on purpose.


End of summary.
